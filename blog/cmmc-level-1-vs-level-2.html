CMMC Level 1 vs Level 2:
Which Do You Actually Need?

A clear decision framework based on your contract flow-down requirements, the type of information you handle, and what each level actually requires in terms of effort and cost.

The most expensive mistake a defense contractor can make in CMMC preparation is investing in the wrong level. Level 1 and Level 2 are dramatically different in scope, cost, and effort — and the determining factor is not your company size or revenue. It is the type of information you handle under your DoD contracts.

The Quick Answer

If your contracts involve Controlled Unclassified Information (CUI) — technical data, engineering drawings, test results, source code for government systems, or any information marked with a CUI category — you need CMMC Level 2. If you only handle Federal Contract Information (FCI) — basic contract correspondence, invoices, and administrative data that is not CUI — Level 1 is sufficient.

How to Determine Your Required Level

Step 1: Check your contracts. Look for DFARS 252.204-7012 — if this clause appears, your contract involves CUI and you need Level 2. If you only see FAR 52.204-21, Level 1 may be sufficient. If you have multiple contracts, your highest requirement applies.

Step 2: Identify your data types. Do you receive or generate technical data, specifications, source code, test results, or operational information for DoD systems? That is almost certainly CUI. Do you only send invoices and status reports? That is likely FCI only.

Step 3: Talk to your contracting officer. If there is any ambiguity, ask. Getting a written clarification from your CO is far cheaper than preparing for the wrong level.

Important: Subcontractor Flow-Down

If you are a subcontractor, your prime contractor's CMMC requirement flows down to you if you handle the same type of information. A prime with Level 2 requirements who shares CUI with you means you also need Level 2. Do not assume your prime has scoped you out — verify in writing.

The Cost Difference is Real

Level 1 is achievable for most small businesses with existing IT staff and a modest consulting engagement. The 17 practices are basic cyber hygiene — things like using antivirus, limiting physical access, and requiring passwords. Most organizations are already partially compliant without realizing it.

Level 2 is a fundamentally different undertaking. The 110 controls across 14 families require a formal security program, documented policies for every control family, a complete System Security Plan, and evidence artifacts that demonstrate implementation. For a company without an existing security program, this can mean significant investments in both technology and consulting services.

The worst outcome is preparing for Level 1 when you need Level 2 — you spend time and money, feel a false sense of compliance, and then discover at RFP time that you are 12 months away from the certification you actually need.
