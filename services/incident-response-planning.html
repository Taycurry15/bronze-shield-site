The Problem

Template IRPs Fail Under Pressure

An IRP that sits in a binder and has never been tested is worse than no plan at all — it creates a false sense of readiness. When an incident occurs, your team reaches for the plan, finds generic procedures that don't match their tools or team structure, and improvises under pressure. That improvisation leads to delayed containment, poor evidence preservation, and notification timeline violations.

We build incident response documentation that your team can actually execute. Every procedure references your real monitoring tools, your real escalation contacts, and your real decision authorities. And we pressure-test every plan with tabletop exercises before signing off.

What We Deliver

IRP Development Process

A structured engagement that produces a living document, not a compliance artifact.

Environment Assessment

We inventory your monitoring tools, communication channels, team structure, clearance levels, and existing SOC/CIRT workflows. The IRP must fit your operational reality, not a theoretical model.

Role-Based Playbooks

Specific, step-by-step procedures for each role in the incident response process — ISSM, system admin, network engineer, program manager, legal, and communications. Each person knows exactly what they do and in what order.

Escalation Matrix & Contact Lists

Decision authority thresholds, notification timelines, and current contact information for every escalation level — from initial detection through AO notification and external reporting requirements (US-CERT, DIBNET). Includes the 72-hour reporting requirement for cyber incidents per DFARS 252.204-7012, with mandatory submission through the DIBNet portal.

Tabletop Exercise

Every IRP engagement includes at least one facilitated tabletop exercise using a scenario relevant to your threat profile. We identify gaps in the plan, measure response times, and refine procedures based on what we observe.
